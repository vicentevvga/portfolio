Disney Animation: The Illusion of Life (1981)

Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston, Chapter 1 & 2

[23-07-24]

Contrary to all my personal beliefs, I bought a physical copy of a book, and none other that "The Illusion of Life", written by legendary Disney Wise Old Men, Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston. A book that needs no introduction other than saying this is probably the most important material for students and animation enthusiasts. The text achieves this by telling the history of the Walt Disney Studio and explaining how the people who worked there developed the techniques for giving their characters the illusion of life.

I've been reading the .pdf version since I knew about its existence around 3 years ago but I've never finished it. That was the case until my Character Animation teacher recently highlighted the importance of this text and its usefulness for learning the arts of animation, ranking it even higher than classics like Richard Williams's or Eric Goldberg's animation handbooks. I decided then that I wanted to give it a proper reading from cover to cover, and here I am.

Since I received the physical copy, I've read the first two chapters so far, so I'll proceed to give a little summary of them. One feature of the physical version that I personally like is the possibility to check in detail all the beautiful illustrations and animation examples the book has.

Chapter 1, titled "An Art Form Is Born", begins by describing the humankind's search to develop art and to find ways to give movement and life to its creations. Starting from the cave paintings in southwest Europe to the first uses of film and camera, the authors describe how animation was born and all the challenges it initially faced. Despite the possibilities of this new media were infinite, the artists needed to learn how to communicate and make the audience emotionally involve with these drawings. The solution to this was giving personality to these fictional characters, and making people care about their feelings.

According to Thomas and Johnston, Walt Disney was one of the first entertainers who was commited to express more complex and meaningful ideas in animation. Together with his brother Roy, they made the efforts to push the boundaries of the medium by founding an animation studio and experimenting with new techniques that might resonate with the public.

Chapter 2, "The Early Days 1923-1933", delves into the story of the Disney Brothers and their first endeavors in the entertainment industry. I don't think many people are aware of how different was the medium at that age. Animation back then did not try to imitate real life nor caricature it. The technique was very limited and there was no other intention in the media but to get quick laughs through simple gags.

Walt Disney encouraged his staff to try different approaches to animation by using bottom pegs in the light tables and manually rolling through the pages to give fluidity to the drawings. Test shooting also gave the animators the possibility of checking their drafts and experimenting more. Disney employees studied live-action comedians and discussed how to properly stage the acting of the characters. All these efforts led their productions to stand out from their competitors and expand what was possible for animation.

Reading these chapters helped me to consolidate certain notions I had already learned about this animation history, and also taught me more about the philosophy and fundamentals behind this impressive craftsmanship.

It surprised me especially how little was animation valued at times, and I feel privileged to live in a age where so many amazing productions have been made. In this economic crisis and uncertainty we are facing, I believe it's important for artists to keep protecting their art so that animation can sustain its reputation among the audience.

I think anyone who works or wants to be part of the industry can benefit from taking a look at the words these wise old men have for us. We need to be aware of the evolution and efforts that a many people have made to reach the point we are at, so we can keep working toward more impressive productions and discover new ways to connect with people through art and passion.

    Red Car, Blue Car (1989)

    Milky Way's animated advertisement

    [16-07-24]

    "The Red Car and the Blue Car had a race..." those are the starting lyrics in this charming Milky Way animated advertisement from the United Kingdom. The successfull 40-second ad features two fearless cars racing in the middle of the desert to get from fictional place "Lunchville" into, also non-existant, "Dinnertown".

    In the meantime, the evil Red Car carelessly chews everything in its path, gaining some weight in the process, while the smart Blue Car takes a Milky Way bar. In the climax of the race, the competitors face a road ending with a big cliff in front. The Red Car falls into this put, while the Blue one easily jumps across the canyon thanks to the energy provided by the chocolate bar, thus winning the race.

    The context behind this ad begins between 1988 and 1989 when English company Mars Inc., hired The2Rogers advertisement agency to develop a commercial promoting its famous Milky Way bar. The agency then developed the main concept for the clip and asked Paul Vester's Speedy Films to produce the animation, and musician Mike Connaris (Mcasso Productions) to compose the song. The ad was directed by Barry Baker.

    The animation technique used is traditional hand-drawn cel animation. The drawings are highly stylized, with beautiful lines, detailed textures, and use perspective in a interesting way. Each frame is carefully shaded as if it were painted using airbrushes. The backgrounds, done by Mark Shepherd, display beautiful desert landscapes with an American "Route 66" aesthetic. The music supports the action by narrating all the milestones in the story.

    The animated short was finally released in the UK television in 1989 and it was an instant hit. The ad was extremely well received by the audience and launched two spinoffs in the following years. The commercial was even re-released 20 years later with no changes at all, being fondly remembered by people all over the UK. The catchy country song in this commercial went so popular that ended releasing music singles and peaking in Billboard charts of that era.

    This little piece of animation is an important milestone in the English advertising history.

    In the words of its director "Probably the best known of all the adverts I directed", and I easily see that. Watching this ad feels like reading a short but funny fairy tale, and I like it because of that.

    A Greenthumb is Not a Goldfinger (1970)

    "Josie and The Pussycats" animated episode

    [10-07-24]

    "Josie and The Pussycats" was an animated show that aired on CBS from 1970 to 1972, consisting of just one season with 16 episodes. It was based on a popular comic series with the same name, which was part of Archie Comics. The show was aimed at teens and featured Jose, Valerie, and Melody, three young musicians who fought against villains while advancing in their rock and roll career.

    "A Greenthumb is Not a Goldfinger", a title reference to the famous James Bond movie, was the second episode of the show. It was directed by William Hanna and Joseph Barbera and aired on September 19, 1970.

    The plot of this episode starts when Alexandra, a friend of the band but a rival of Josie, schemes to send the gang to the Amazonian jungle on a cargo flight, away from their intended destination, Nashville. In the rainforest they meet Dr. Greenstone and discovers that the man is creating dangerous biological plants to blackmail other countries for money. They are captured by Greenstone's guards but manage to escape into a nearby village.

    In this village, they meet the "head-hunters", a dangerous local tribe who try to kill the party. After running away from them, the crew goes back to Greenstone's lair and stop the villain's plan in a silly chasing sequence in Scooby-Doo style. At the end of the episode, the Pussycats perform a song, and the credits rolls.

    The technique used in this episode is traditional cel animation. Nevertheless, it is quite easy to see that this production did not have the best budget to spend. The animation is limited; the character usually just moves their mouths while speaking and stand still the rest of the time. The camera angles stays mostly the same, and there are not interesting shots or cinematography.

    The writing is also simple and the dialogues are straightforward. The characters are mostly one-dimensional and state exactly what are they thinking at the time. Even villians like Alexandra and Dr. Greenstone speaks directly to the audience in order to reveal their plans.

    All these aspects mentioned were common features of American Saturday-morning cartoons of that era. Television boom demanded fast content, and studios like Hanna-Barbera managed to produce many animated series without expending valuable resources.

    Despite all those flaws, "Josie and The Pussycats" became a cultural icon, being named one of the #100 best animated series by IGN. The show, and it music, was a success, sharing elements with similar series like "The Archie Show" and "Scooby-Doo". "A Greenthumb is Not a Goldfinger" scores 7.4 out of 10 on the IMBd rating system.

    The Pink Phink (1964)

    Animated short film directed by Friz Freleng

    [02-07-24]

    I remember watching "The Pink Panther" episodes on television when I was a kid. I've always found the panther character quite stylish and interesting, but I've never researched his origins before.

    For those reasons, I picked this Friz Freleng clip from 1964 for this week's review. It turns out this short was the first appereance of the pink character after starring in the intro of Blake Edward's comedy film. The movie features a little man who is trying to accomplish his job of painting a house blue, while the Pink Panther tries to do the opposite by painting the same house pink.

    The technique in this short is traditional cel animation. Although Friz Freleng had previous experience working on Looney Tunes movies, this short presents a more elegant aesthetic, with stylish designs and simple backgrounds.

    It is quite easy to spot the influence of the UPA animation style from the 50s in the backgrounds, use of color, and geometric shapes displayed in the short. The jazz music also supports the mood and actions the characters perform.

    The basic premise of this film might perfectly fit in a Bugs vs. Elmer plot in any Merrie Melodies scene, but The Pink Phink's gags are resolved in a completely different way. Visual jokes are predominant, and there is less violence and wackiness compared to the Bugs Bunny gang.

    As stated before, "The Pink Phink" was released in 1964 and won the Academy Award for Best Animated Short that year. It also helped to launch an animated series featuring the character, which broadcast between 1969 and 1978. The short has been been praised by many people in the animation industry and has been referenced in different parodies and shows (my favorite one is Dexter's Laboratory).

    Rabbit of Seville (1950)

    Animated short film directed by Chuck Jones

    [25-06-24]

    When I was searching for a short to review, I immediately knew it had to be “Rabbit of Seville” as soon as I recognized the thumbnail. It has so many iconic jokes and scenes that I felt a strong need to learn more about it.

    This 7-minute Looney Tunes short film was directed by the legendary Chuck Jones and written by Michael Maltese. It features the characters Bugs Bunny and Elmer Fudd, performing different gags during a play based on the famous opera "The Barber of Seville".

    The technique in this short is traditional cel animation with the distinctive wacky style of Looney Tunes, relying mainly on physical comedy and absurd logic. The action is well synchronized with the musical score by Carl Stalling, who arranges several opera pieces and support the gags. Bugs Bunny even sings during specific parts of the music, which results in a experience that results so funny to watch.

    One of the most distinctive elements in this film is the animation quality. The barber scene when Bugs shaves Elmer's scalp (despite him being bald) and gives him a head massage with his finger and toes at the same time is impressive and hilarious. You can feel the solid body animation principle perfectly executed by the Warner Bros team.

    The plot in this short starts with a chase sequence of Bugs and Elmer running into a theatre, just before the intended play is about to begin. Bugs Bunny then assummes the role of Figaro character in this play and starts messing with Elmer while he sings opera and performs in a comedic way. The intensity of the action keeps increasing until Bugs and Elmer get involved in a shaving-chair challenge and skyrocket into the sky. The movie ends with the usual fourth-wall breaking of Bugs speaking to the camera.

    The short film was released in theaters on December 16, 1950, and it's been praised by many animator enthusiasts. In 1994 "Rabbit of Seville"was ranked in #12 in "The 50 Greatest Cartoons" book, where animation historian Greg Ford highlighted its non-stop slapstick humor. It is important to recognize the source of inspiration this short was for "What's Opera, Doc", one of the greatest shorts in animation history. And, yes. Drag-Bugs makes and appereance, of course.

    Working with (Walt) Disney

    Interviews with Disney Artists by Don Peri

    [17-06-24]

    This week I've selected two interviews from "Working with Walt (2008)" and "Working with Disney (2011)" books by Disney historian Don Peri. It's been very fun. The more I read about the history of animation, the more my passion for the discipline grows. All the following information was found in Don Peri's books.

    Les Clark (1907 – 1979)

    Leslie James "Les" Clark was one of the most important Disney animators of the classical era. He was born in Utah, 1907, and was hired by Walt Disney after showing him his drawings in a local restaurant. Les Clark started working for the company just two days after graduating from high school, and stayed in the studio for the next forty-eight years.

    During his career in Disney he worked as inker, inbetweener, animator and director. He worked in features like "Pinocchio", "Dumbo", "Peter Pan", "Lady and the Tramp", among many others classical films.

    I choose Les Clark because I wanted to learn more about one of Disney's Nine Old Men, and I believe he is certainly one of the first of them in joining the studio.

    By reading the interview conducted by Don Peri I found specially interesting the relation that Les Clark had with Walt Disney and his commitment with his view. You cannot deny the admiration that Clark had over Disney, staying loyal to him even during the animator's strike of 1940.

    For me, by far the deepest moment of Don Peri interview was when he asked Les why he has that strong connection with Mikey Mouse character and he simply aswered "He was Walt to me".

    Walter Lantz (1900 – 1994)

    Walter Benjamin Lantz was an American cartoonist and artist, born in 1899. He is famously known for the creation of like "Woody Woodpecker" character and for running his own animation studio.

    He started his carreer at sixteen, creating cartoons for popular comic strips and writing gags. He worked for Universal and then he founded "Walter Lanz Productions" where he produced and directed many short animation films and shows. He retired in 1972 and received an honorary Oscar award in 1979.

    I would like to highlight the part in Don Peri's interview when Lantz stated that there was a genuine friendship among animation studios during the clasical era of animation. Walter Lantz explained that people from Warner Bros, MGM, Disney and himself usually kept good relations and wanted the better for the industry.

    I personally picked Mr. Lantz because I remember with nostalgia those Woody Woodpecker cartoons I watched as kid and Walter Lantz interventions where he showed these little documentaries about how classic cartoons where made.

    You Ought to Be in Pictures (1940)

    Animated short fim directed by Friz Freleng

    [16-06-24]

    This week I decided to watch “You Ought to Be in Pictures (1940),” a 9-minute Looney Tunes short film directed by Friz Freleng and produced by Leon Schlesinger. The story features Porky Pig resigning to his role as a cartoon character and his attempt to break into films as a Hollywood actor.

    The most distintive element of this movie is the mix of live action with traditional hand-drawn animation. The animated characters of this short (Porky Pig and Daffy Duck) interact with real footage of Hollywood studios and living actors.

    Although this was not the first time that animated productions attempted this technique (we have famous examples from "Gertie the Dinasour" to "Alice Comedies" series by Disney) I believe "You Ought to Be in Pictures" manages to combines these elements with high quality and effectiveness.

    The plot of this story starts with Duffy Duck convincing Porky to resign from his job as a cartoon character to pursue a carreer in live-action feature films. Duffy's actions were probably driven by his jelausy against Porky's fame. Consequently Porky meets with the head of the studio, Leon Schlesinger, who agrees to end Porky's contract and wishes him good luck.

    Porky then leaves the studio and tries to break into some film sets to be part a production. Unfortunately, his attempts go wrong, so the charismatic pig decides to go back to Mr. Schlesinger and request his old job back. In the meantime, Daffy Duck has been trying to persuade Schlesinger for giving him the lead cartoon role that Porky used to have.

    By the end, Porky Pig is welcomed by his former boss and it is revealed to him that his contract was never torned up, so he happily goes back to keep doing his regular job.

    The short film was released in theaters in 1940. I'm truly delighted with how well the mix of cartoons and live action was done in this film, giving it a meta-narrative trait that would be more explored in future movies like "Who Framed Roger Rabbit? (1998)" and "Space Jam (1996)". I couldn't find any original reviews of the theatrical release of this short, but several comments on the internet (IMBD and Mubi) addresses the fact that the short works more as a technical demo rather than a funny Looney Tunes experience. I agree with the latter.

    The Wizard of Oz (1933)

    Animated short fim directed by Ted Eshbaugh

    [04-06-24]

    This week's movie review is “The Wizard of Oz (1933),” an animated 8-minute short film directed by Ted Eshbaugh. The story depicts the well-known story of Dorothy, a girl from Kansas, and her dog Toto who are taken by a tornado and transported to the magical land of Oz.

    The animation of this films fits the rubber-hose style, with each drawing lasting for just one frame and presenting exaggerated movements in the characters. The character designs might look similar to Disney's at first glance, but they present subtle anatomical differences and body shapes. You can tell by the watercolor technique in the backgrounds that this film was animated on paper and then captured by layers using cel animation.

    One of the most disctinctive characteristics of this movie is the presence of color. It is certainly amazing the moment when Dorothy travels to Oz and you can see literal shift in tone the film displays, transitioning from a monochromatic green to a diverse landscape with yellows, greens and more. Unfortunatly the theatrical release of this short was in grayscale due to legal troubles with Technicolor, and the version I watched on YouTube displays the colors originally intented. Altough the film doesn't present dialoges it does features synchronized sound and a musical successfully. The score was again arranged by legendary musician Carl W. Stalling.

    The plot of this story follows Dorothy and Toto across the yellow brick road toward Emerald City. In the meantime they meet the Thin Man and the Scarecrow, who happily join their party (and no, the Lion is not present in this short). Once they reach the city they are cheerfully welcomed by the wacky inhabitants who present a big parade for their visitors. Finally the protagonists meet the famous Wizard of Oz who performs different magical tricks to his guests, until one of those tricks goes gring and the entire party must work together to stop de menace. The entire movie it's full of little slapstick gags.

    The short film was released in theaters in June 1933. In my opinion the movie manages to effectively convey the charm of the world presented in the book despite being produced six year before the legendary live action version. I couldn't find reviews about the reception of the theatrical release but nowdays the movie is considered a cult classic, way ahead of it's time specially because the novelty in the use of color.     Hell's Bells (1929)

    Drawn by Ub Iwerks and produced by Walt Disney

    [29-05-24]

    My film selection for this week is “Hell’s Bells,” a Silly Symphony animated short released in theaters in 1929. The piece was directed and drawn by Disney animator Ub Iwerks (Steamboat Willie, The Skeleton Dance), and produced by Walt Disney. The 6-minute story features a series of creatures from the underworld performing little gags, commanded by Satan himself.

    The animation technique fits the classical rubber-hose style of that age, presenting flexible characters and an absurd sense of humor. The animation was done on paper, inked and painted on celluloid frames, and shot in gray scale, with watercolor backgrounds. [1]

    Although Disney productions did not use a multiplane camera at that time, the short does a couple of perspective tricks to add depth to the scenery, such as drawing characters taking a step closer into the audience and using the camera zooming for dramatic effect.

    The film also features synchronized sound and a musical score that supports the action and little choreographs that the characters performs, arranged by famous musician Carl W. Stalling.

    The first minutes of the short present low-profile characters doing silly dances and activities, acting with a spooky but funny attitude. These creatures belong to classical hellish depictions, like the three headed dog, a giant spider, and a lizard roaming around the place.

    The second half of “Hell’s Bells” focuses on The Devil, ruler of this nightmare world, and his attempt to feed little to his infernal dog with his demon minions. By the end, one of those devils escapes from his master and, after a chase, manages to defeat The Devil by sending him over a cliff into the flames of hell.

    The film was distributed by Columbia Pictures and exhibited in different cinemas starting November 1920. The reception of this film was mixed, being compared with the more successful short “The Skeleton Dance”, released the same year. “Hell’s Bells” couldn’t manage to surpass any expectations over what was done before.     Why I'm interested in animation

    And who can possibly tell

    [21-05-24]

    I’m pursuing a career in animation because I believe it is a discipline that provides me with an appealing and practical way to tell the stories I care about. I personally don’t think that animation is the definitive art form, but it is certainly a narrative language that I truly enjoy and love.

    But why not cinema, theatre, or literature then? I think it must be related to my personal journey and context. Cartoon television from the 90s and early 2000s was a big part of my childhood and adolescence. I had the privilege to watch an excellent generation of stories and shows provided by studios like Cartoon Network and Nickelodeon, and even high-quality anime broadcasted on local Chilean TV Channels.

    I also had a special connection with drawing and doodling as a kid, which developed into a special interest in characters, caricatures, aesthetics, and visual communication in general. I’ve always been interested in technology advances and learning new tools to express my ideas, and animation is an excellent field for addressing that curiosity.

    If I had to pick three animated pieces that resonates especially with me, I would say “The Simpsons”, “Samurai Jack” and “SpongeBob SquarePants”.

    Matar a un hombre (2014)

    Así, tal cual

    [28-12-20]

    "Matar a un hombre", en lo personal, me llega como una obra de oposición. Protesta contra el formato mac-donalístico que estamos acostumbrados a consumir en los thrillers policiacos que hacen los gringos, o en los dramas que se basan en la exageración para causar emoción.

    La película es lenta. Planos largos. Poca música incidental y pocos arrebatos emocionales. No obstante, me parece que explota precisamente aquellas injusticias de nuestro sistema que son, precisamente, sin glamour.

    Poner una denuncia en Carabineros. Esperar en una sala, hablar con un fiscal. Asistir a una audiencia. Declarar. Esperar que algo pase. Presenciar cómo tu familia es acosada y amenazada por una fuerza ajena que nadie puede confrontar. Volver a la oficina a preguntar. Caballero, no hay nada más que yo pueda hacer ahora. Vuelva más tarde.

    No es necesario acudir a metáforas simbólicas para lograr la catarsis. La burocracia, la mezquinidad política y la ley del "más vío" superan cualquier ficción: constituyen la realidad cotidiana del 99% de Chile. Son procesos sistemáticos, complejos, que no se van a resolver con una pelea épica del jovencito contra el villano, escapando de un edifico en llamas con la jovencita en sus brazos.

    Alejandro Fernández Almendras (AFA) es un kapo del cine del (casi) etnográfico. Admiro cómo ha sabido contar estas historias sin echarle ketchup a un plato que ya es, de por sí, súper atractivo. Quedo ansioso, y expectante, de seguir viendo las próximas cosas que vaya sacando.

    Digitalipsis #1

    Fantasías, deseos, ambiciones

    [25-12-20]

    Un lienzo en blanco, pero no tan blanco. Mejor en negro, con colores de neón RGB y retrofurisimo. Un espacio más que permita profundizar en extremos, cual obra de ciencia ficción. Presentar opiniones fuertes frente a temas superficiales (obsesión millennial) y apuntar a los límites.

    Me gustan los monos. Las fantasías. Las aventuras. Sería lindo poder ridiculizar la realidad todavía más. Diseñar personajes, inventarles palabras, una voz. Contradicciones, deseos y frustraciones. Arrojarles a un coliseo romano y observar cómo adquieren vida para preservar lo que tienen. El día siguiente al apocalipsis tiene toda la pinta de ser el escenario ideal.

    No me siento instruido y espero nunca serlo. En el camino quiero equivocarme, pero equivocarme jugando. No esconderse en una cajita de "shrödinguer" (ni cagando se escribe así) gozando de la cómoda ambigüedad. Mojarse el potito, pero sin darse color. Agarrar lo que vaya resonando más, darle una vuelta y materializarlo en discos duros y transistores. Política, filosofía, biología, teoría del color. No importa lo que se venga pa que sepas que te quiero.

    Aprovechar lo multimedia y lo interdisciplinario. Estar siempre con las antenitas paradas, atento a copiar lo que vaya sirviendo y trastocándolo. Estímulos siempre van a existir, pero inevitablemente vamos filtrando en base a lo que nos llama la atención. Como estas guaguas que miran todo cuando adquieren noción de la realidad.

    Y cuando se logré ¿qué? No sé si se puede pensar en ese día de forma tan concreta. Ojalá que pa ese entonces haya más cosas con la que entretenerse. Seguramente sí. Que valgan la pena las calorías quemadas y el espacio ocupado en estas vueltas al sol.

    Cuneta Food

    Apuntes culinarios antes de que Santiago explotara

    30-11-2018

    En mi calidad de peatón recurrente (que tiende a sobrepensar las weás más allá de lo conveniente), concluí la otra vez que conservo en mi cerebrito una serie de reflexiones respecto a la Comida de Cuneta santiaguina que podría valer la pena compartir.

    Sumándole el hecho de que no como carne (y que evito comer cualquier derivado animal), el desafío de alimentarse en sectores populares se vuelve particularmente entretenido. Además el centro está lleno de vida inmigrante, lo que le da una frescura a lo que se podía pillar hace unos años. Aquí va mi vomitada de reflexiones:

    COMIDAS

    1. TEQUEÑOS: buenos, bonitos y baratos. No les veo un sabor muy distinto a una empanada de queso frita, pero su formato es más agradable de comer, y se pueden compartir con lxs amigxs. Además los venden en unas bolsitas de papel muy agradables de transportar. Mi carro favorito está en Curicó con Santa Rosa. El caballero vende 5 por luca y tiene salsas venezolanas que no son muy comunes de pillar (por ejemplo, de guayaba y de piña ñamiñami)

    2. CACHAPAS: estas weás son demasiado ricas. Grotescamente buenas. La primera vez que las probé, una señora chilena me dijo que ella había caminado cuadras especialmente pa bajonear ahí también. Le creí totalmente. Pueden salir más de una luca, pero definitivamente te quitan el hambre (y sin ser tan pesadas porque son de harina de maiz). Lo malo para la sensibilidad vegana: tienen demasiado queso. Probablemente sea más dañino para el animal que una tajada de carne. Las cachapas no se venden mucho en la calle porque son un poco complicadas de preparar, pero hay un carrito en Ñuble con Vicuña que se pone regularmente.

    3. PERRO CALIENTE: nunca he comido porque tienen salchichas. De todas formas, es interesante que los venezolanos no hayan enganchado con los completos chilenos, y hayan tenido que imponer su versión del Hot Dog. En ese sentido me cargan. Son unos panes fome con vienesa y ketchup.

    4. FALÁFEL: la única comida árabe que he probado en la calle además de los dulces árabes. Como son de garbanzo, son ideales para tener una rica proteína mezclada con verduras dentro de una masita. Los tkm harto. Hay un compadre que se pone en Serrano con Alameda que los vende a $1.500. Bien buenos.

    5. ASADOS: los odio en forma y fondo. Más allá de la carne, encuentro terrible el humo y el olor fuerte que tiran. Me cuesta pensar cómo puede ser apetitoso para alguien que va saliendo del trabajo, con 25°C, acercarse a esas parrillas a comprarse un anticucho (posiblemente de perro). Y estos carros tienen una fijación importante con la Alameda, asi que es difícil arrancar. Prefiero que me fumen en la cara.

    6. SOPAIPILLAS: siguen siendo lo máximo. Lejos son la alternativa más barata, y su relación calorías/plata es insuperable. Son veganos, y la posibilidad de echarles mostaza, ketchup o pebre les da versatilidad. Me da la sensación de que cada vez veo menos, eso sí.

    SECTORES DESTACABLES

    1. CURICÓ ENTRE LIRA Y SANTA ROSA: Patio de comidas venezolano-cuneta. En un fin de semana se pueden llegar a poner 15 puestos, con distintas cosas. Es bonito. Ponen música y se siente mucho el ambiente de fraternidad entre los migrantes. El espacio es agradable y nunca he visto que los fiscalicen (en una de esas tienen permiso).

    2. PASEO PUENTE Y BANDERA CON CATEDRAL: esta manzana es un sector totalmente peruano. Venden muchas tortas y pasteles por trocitos. Nunca les he comprado porque algún rechazo me genera ver que tengan las tortas abiertas ahí, totalmente descubiertas. Lo terrible es que también andan con unos carros de supermercados donde arman la fritanga de pollo con papas fritas. Esa sí que es desagradable de pasar. A veces hacen una especie de callejón en la vereda y tenis que pasar entre medio. Hago lo posible por hacerles el quite.

    3. GAM, salida del metro: es conocido que ese sector tiene muchos bajones de comida vegana. Desde hamburguesas de legumbres hasta handroll de champiñones. Es barato también. Lo fome es que hay harto joven chileno universitario con inquietudes posmodernas y sin mucha necesidad económica (como yo). Bacán que se hagan sus monedas, pero me caen mejor lxs migrantes.
